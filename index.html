Why Multisensory?

Students with severe dyslexia or other learning disabilities often require hundreds of correct repetitions of a skill to form new neural pathways. Repetition done correctly creates changes at the synaptic level, strengthening the connections between neurons so that reading becomes automatic. The number of repetitions needed increases dramatically for struggling readers — while a typical student may need 4–14 exposures, a student with dyslexia may need 40–200 repetitions to master the same skill. These drills are most effective when they are multisensory: they must involve sight, sound and movement together. Because tablets and computers lack the ability to capture and guide gross motor and tactile actions, current reading apps cannot facilitate the precise, kinesthetic drills that reading specialists deliver, leaving the most affected students underserved.

  • Exact repetition & kinesthetic drills: reading specialists deliver structured drills that must be repeated with precision. These drills engage the whole body — tracing letters, tapping out phonemes and forming words with manipulatives — and cannot be fully replicated on a flat touchscreen.
  • Sky writing: students draw letters in the air using gross motor movements. Holding the arm straight and tracing large letters links sound and muscle memory, creating pathways in the brain.
  • Tactile surfaces: tracing letters or words in sand, rice, felt or velvet while saying the sounds aloud builds kinesthetic memory and engages touch.
  • Whole‑body movement: hopping out syllables or gesturing tricky sounds gets students moving and keeps attention focused. When children are taught using all three pathways to the brain, retention increases and learning becomes more enjoyable.
